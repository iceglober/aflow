At 200+ claims/month, unit economics are strong. Alternatively, per-claim pricing ($1-2/claim) positions below the $25-117 cost of manual rework per denied claim — easy ROI argument.

### Gap Analysis — Where We Fit

No incumbent combines all three:
1. **Automated 837D generation from PMS data** (clearinghouses require practices to format and submit)
2. **RPA fallback for non-EDI payers** (clearinghouses drop claims they can't route electronically)
3. **AI-powered attachment collection and formatting** (manual in every workflow today)

Clearinghouses are pipes — they transport claims but don't create or scrub them. Outsourcers use humans to do what automation should. We automate the creation-to-submission pipeline and use both EDI and RPA to guarantee delivery to any payer.

## 5. Business Model Patterns

### Pattern 1: Per-Practice SaaS (dominant for embedded tools)
- Monthly subscription ($99-300/practice)
- Predictable revenue, easy to sell through channel partner
- Partner takes 20-30% revenue share (standard for embedded dental software)
- Risk: practices churn if claim volume is low

### Pattern 2: Per-Claim Transaction Fee
- $1-3 per claim submitted
- Revenue scales with practice volume
- More aligned with value delivered
- Risk: revenue volatility, harder to forecast

### Pattern 3: Percentage of Collections (outsourcing model)
- 4-10% of insurance collections
- Highest revenue per practice ($3K-8K/month for average practice)
- Requires full RCM scope (beyond our current scope of submission only)
- Risk: collection risk, revenue recognition complexity

### Pattern 4: Freemium + Upsell
- Free claim submission (eat Stedi costs as acquisition tool)
- Monetize on premium features: attachment automation, denial prevention, analytics
- Risk: high CAC, slow payback

**Recommended model for launch**: Per-practice SaaS ($150-250/month) sold through channel partner with revenue share. Simple to operationalize, predictable, and aligned with partner's existing per-practice pricing model for eligibility. Per-claim pricing as an alternative for high-volume DSO prospects outside the partner channel.

## 6. Distribution Channels

### Primary: Channel Partner (11K practices)
- Embedded app in partner's dental CRM (iframe)
- Partner flips switch to activate claim submission (same as eligibility activation model)
- Partner owns sales relationship; we provide the platform
- Revenue share: negotiate 70/30 or 80/20 (us/partner)
- Advantage: zero direct sales cost, instant distribution to 11K practices

### Secondary: Direct-to-DSO
- DSOs (39% of practices by 2026) have centralized procurement
- API-first approach appeals to DSO tech teams
- Higher ACV per deal (multi-location)
- Requires direct sales motion

### Tertiary: PMS Marketplace
- Open Dental has a marketplace/integration ecosystem
- Eaglesoft/Dentrix (Henry Schein One) — Henry Schein owns 70% of eAssist, potential conflict
- Lower priority — PMS integrations are necessary for the product but not the primary GTM channel

### Channel Risk
Henry Schein's 70% ownership of eAssist creates a potential competitive conflict if we pursue Eaglesoft/Dentrix integration as a distribution channel. The channel partner relationship is the defensible moat — direct PMS distribution faces incumbent resistance.
